# simloom: FoundationDB-style testing for asyncio

> **Draft for the owner to edit.** Numbers below are reproduced by the test
> suite on every CI run; re-verify the landscape claims before publishing.

Your asyncio code is tested one interleaving at a time — the polite one your
laptop happens to schedule. Races ship. Flakes get shrugged at. "Works on my
machine" is the state of the art.

Rust has loom, turmoil, madsim, and shuttle. .NET had Coyote. FoundationDB
built a company-defining simulator and Antithesis sells the methodology at
the hypervisor level. Python — the language a huge share of agent
orchestration and backend glue is written in — had nothing.

simloom is deterministic simulation testing for asyncio, as a `pip install`.

## What it does

Wrap an ordinary async test:

```python
@simloom.test(runs=2000)
async def test_lease_exclusivity():
    ...  # your real, unmodified asyncio code + plain asserts
```

simloom runs it inside a fully simulated universe — a seeded scheduler owns
every interleaving decision, the clock is virtual (an hour of simulated
traffic takes milliseconds), the network is in-memory with tape-driven
latency, loss, partitions, and resets, and hosts crash like real power cuts
(no `finally` blocks, unsynced writes lost or torn). It explores thousands
of schedules, and when one breaks your invariant:

```
FAILED test_lease_exclusivity — simloom found a failing universe
  seed: 17   (re-run: pytest -k lease --simloom-seed=17)
  error: AssertionError: two holders of an exclusive lease
  shrunk: 31 draws -> 29, schedule deviations 25 -> 1 (106 candidate runs)
  minimal schedule: FIFO everywhere except:
    draw #0: sched.pick = 1 (of 4)
  artifacts: .sim/failures/test_lease_exclusivity-seed17.tape.json, ...
```

That seed replays the failure byte-for-byte, forever. The shrinker reduces
the schedule to the minimal deviation from normal execution — above, the
entire bug is "one callback ran second-oldest-first, once."

## Why asyncio makes this possible

Every interleaving decision in asyncio happens at an `await`, under a
replaceable event loop. A custom loop owns every scheduling choice without
forking CPython, without a hypervisor, without recompiling. And because the
ecosystem (aiohttp, httpx, asyncpg, the streams API) bottoms out in loop
primitives, **unmodified real libraries run in-sim**: our CI runs a real
aiohttp server against a real httpx client over the simulated network, with
20% loss injected, replayable from a seed.

Everything nondeterministic flows through one seeded choice tape — the
Hypothesis trick, applied to schedules. Replay and shrinking come from the
same mechanism. Anything that would touch the real world (sockets, signals,
subprocesses, foreign threads) raises `EscapedSimulationError` at the call
site instead of silently breaking determinism.

## It finds real bugs

**A historical CPython race.** Pre-3.12 `asyncio.wait_for` could swallow a
delivered cancellation when the inner future completed in the same window as
the cancel (bpo-42130) — a race that lurked for years and was reported from
production. In production it needed an exact wall-clock collision; under
simloom the timeout boundary is a first-class choice point, and exploration
hands you the interleaving as a seed. The modern implementation survives the
identical torture. (`examples/bpo42130.py`)

**The canonical demo.** A toy Raft over the simulated network, persisted
state and all, tortured with partitions, crashes, and restarts. Plant the
classic double-vote bug and exploration elects two leaders in one term in
roughly one seed out of five; the fixed version survives — and coverage
counters prove the faults actually fired. (`examples/toy_raft.py`)

**It found a bug in itself.** Our Raft gate caught the harness logging the
GC's collected-object count — process-global heap state — which broke
replay equality on long runs. The 10,000-seed determinism torture in CI
exists because the harness's central claim deserves the same hostility it
applies to your code.

## Honest numbers, honest limits

Scheduling strategies are measured, not marketed: on our benchmark zoo, a
uniform random walk finds shallow races far better than PCT (395 vs 44 of
400 seeds), while PCT finds the starvation-class bug a random walk
essentially never hits (151 vs **0** of 400). They are complementary; both
ship.

What escapes the simulation is documented, not hidden: blocking C-extension
I/O, direct wall-clock reads, real subprocesses — `docs/determinism.md` is
the honest boundary, including exactly what `PYTHONHASHSEED` does and does
not buy you across processes.

## Where this goes

The event log and tape are versioned public formats: a recorded production
run is, in effect, a tape generated by reality instead of a PRNG. That is
deliberate — the same deterministic core is the substrate for what comes
next.
